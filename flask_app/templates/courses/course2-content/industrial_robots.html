Industrial Robots

Industrial robotics are the foundation and the largest commercial application of robotics technology today. The goal of industrial robotics is to improve productivity, quality, precision and adaptability with minimal costs.

Let's Dive In

Unimate - The First Industrial Robot

The first instance of an industrial robot dates back to 1954, on a filed patent by George Devol on the programmed article transfer. He and Joseph Engelberger partnered up and founded Unimation, the first robot company that put a robot into service. The Unimate was used in 1961 at General Motors for extracting parts from a die-casting machine. The Unimate was an electronically controlled hydraulic heavy-lifting arm with six axes of motion, capable of lifting objects and moving them precisely in a 3D space. The Unimate had a tool at the end of the arm known as the end-effector that it used to perform tasks such as welding or painting. Another breakthrough emerged in 1969 with the Stanford Arm built by the mechanical engineering student working at the Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory, Victor Sheinman. The Stanford Arm was the first all-electric robotic manipulator.

Robotic arms

Robotic arms are also known as robot manipulators, they are programmed and built to execute similar functions to a human arm. They are used to automate execution of repetitive tasks that may require precision or immense strength. They help with safety, as robotic arms are employed in hazardous areas that pose a risk to humans. Manipulators are connected by joints allowing rotational or linear motion. These robotic arms have end effectors at the end designed to perform tasks with great accuracy, depending on their application, tasks include gripping, spinning, welding, painting, etc. These robotic manipulators can be autonomous or controlled manually and can be mobile or fixed to a specific location. The robotic arm was modeled and built in reference to a human arm.

Key Components:

  • Controllers: The controller is the main processor and acts as the brain of the robot, it receives commands from a program.
  • The arm has four major components:
    • Shoulder Joint:  This is the highest load bearing point in the arm and has three degrees of freedom: pitch, yaw and roll. The shoulder has the widest range of motion in the human body, and it is the foundation of most modern robotic arms.
    • Elbow Joint:  Many mechanical elbow joints have been used in robotic arm manufacture. The elbow joint provides extension, retraction, reach around and angular orientation of the wrist and hand.
    • Wrist Joint:  The wrist is a crucial component of even the earliest prototypes, it's the end-effector terminus and it allows the arm to be manipulated in a three-dimensional space.
    • Hand:  This component defines the purpose and capacity of the robotic arm. The hand is a tool for multitasking capable of diverse tasks, it is also the research topic of many higher education institutions.
  • Robotic arms are also composed of drives and sensors.
    • Drives:  These are the motors that provide movement for every part of the robot. They are located in between the joints and regular rotation and linear movement. These move independently from each other at varying speeds and directions.
    • Sensors:  They allow manipulators to react to their environment.

Types of Robotic Arm Configurations

Cartesian Robot - Also known as the gantry robot, is named after the cartesian coordinate system. These robots include three articulating joints programmed to move linearly using the X, Y and Z coordinates. These robots are typically used for picking and placing items on converter belts.

Articulated Robot Arm - These are the most common mechanical robot arms seen in the workplace. These robots are used for welding, material handling, pick-and- place tasks, dispensing and much more. They resemble human arms because of their multiple axes and degrees of freedom. Because of this, they are among the most adaptable, flexible and space saving robotic manipulators, however, they are by nature dangerous and must be equipped with necessary safety features.

Cylindrical Robot Arm - This robot has at least one rotary joint at its base allowing it a rotational motion along its axis. Its axis constitutes a cylindrical coordinate system (up, down and around). This robotic arm is valuable in industries that produce tubes and cylindrical components. Other uses include die casting and spot welding.

SCARA Robots - SCARA is an acronym for Selective Compliance Assembly Robot Arm. These robots work on a three-dimensional axis (X,Y and Z ) and rotary motion, in other words, it combines cartesian and cylindrical movements. SCARA robots are used in biomedical applications, assembly and pelletising.

Polar Robot - Also known as the spherical robot. It consists of an arm with two rotary joints and one linear joint connected to the base with a twisting joint. The axes work together to form a polar coordinate. These are used for injection molding, welding and material handling.

Delta Robot - Delta Robots, or parallel robots, possess three arms connected to a single base, which is mounted above the workspace. Delta Robots work in a dome- shape and can move both delicately and precisely at high speeds due to each joint of the end effector being directly controlled by all three arms.
